## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: In multi-agent Large Language Model (LLM) systems, prompt specifications carry data contracts and integration logic across many interdependent files. However, unlike source code, these natural language specifications are rarely subjected to rigorous structured inspection, often undergoing only informal reviews and incremental updates.
- **Why Existing Work Falls Short**: Traditional software testing and single-file code reviews fail to effectively capture defects in prompt specifications. Design changes in one document often cause ripple effects across others that are easily missed. Existing methods lack structured mechanisms for checking consistency across multiple documents, leading to runtime failures (e.g., wrong field names, stale references) that remain undetected during development.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed**: An Iterative Agent-Driven Auditing framework for quality assurance, applied to the production system AEGIS.
- **Challenge 1: Cross-document consistency defects are missed by single-file reviews**. Since prompt logic is distributed, single-perspective reviews miss cross-file dependency errors. The paper addresses this by using multi-round iterative audits with expanded scopes, employing Claude sub-agents to execute checklist-driven walkthroughs, successfully surfacing defect classes only visible after scope expansion.
- **Challenge 2: Non-monotonic convergence behavior in auditing is hard to predict**. Due to cascading edits and dynamic scope expansion, defect discovery rates do not decrease linearly. The paper conducts nine sequential audit rounds, records per-round defect counts, proposes a seven-category defect taxonomy with coding rules, explains the non-monotonic convergence, and distills a reproducible audit protocol.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation**: The study was conducted on AEGIS (Autonomous Engineering Governance and Intelligence System), a production seven-lane orchestration pipeline. The prompt specification surface comprised approximately 7,150 lines (6,907 lines across seven lane PROMPT.md files and a 245-line shared Ticket Contract). Audits were executed by Claude sub-agents using a checklist-driven walkthrough adapted from Weinberg and Freedman.
- **Evaluation Results**: Over nine consecutive audit rounds, 51 prompt-specification consistency defects were identified (distinct from 51 adversarial code findings in a companion preprint). The per-round defect counts were 15, 8, 12, 2, 8, 1, 4, 1, and 0, achieving zero-defect convergence. The final locked checklist was released as a reproducibility appendix.
- **Comparison**: Compared to traditional single-file reviews, this study demonstrates that single-file reviews missed specific defect classes that were only uncovered in later, expanded-scope rounds. This highlights the superiority of structured, multi-round iterative auditing for capturing errors in complex multi-agent system prompts.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper presents an empirical case study demonstrating the effectiveness of iterative agent-driven auditing in multi-agent LLM systems. It reveals seven common categories of consistency defects in prompt specifications and observes non-monotonic convergence behavior during the audit process. The proposed audit protocol and taxonomy provide concrete guidance for improving quality assurance in prompt engineering, emphasizing the importance of structured, cross-document validation in multi-file environments. While limited to a single system and LLM family, the methodology lays the groundwork for future generalization.

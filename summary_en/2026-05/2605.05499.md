## 1. What the paper did
- **Background**: The widespread adoption of camera-equipped mobile devices and wearables has made capturing meal images for real-time dietary monitoring increasingly practical. However, converting real-world meal photos into reliable dietary logs remains challenging due to significant variations in appearance, portion size, preparation style, lighting, and background.
- **Why existing work fails**: Existing deep learning models (e.g., CNN or Transformer-based discriminative models) perform well in coarse-grained classification but struggle to capture fine-grained attributes like cooking styles, often treating them as independent outputs without ensuring hierarchical consistency. Modern Vision-Language Models (VLMs) offer stronger open-ended reasoning but generate non-canonical labels (such as synonyms, overly specific dish names, or labels outside the dataset taxonomy), which complicates evaluation and practical deployment.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed**: FoodCHA, a multimodal agentic framework that reformulates food recognition as a hierarchical decision-making process, utilizing the compact Moondream-2B vision-language model for efficient reasoning.
- **Challenge 1: Difficulty in fine-grained attribute recognition and lack of hierarchical consistency**. Real-world food images exhibit high intra-class similarity and often contain multiple items, making it hard for models to distinguish subcategories and cooking styles while maintaining logical consistency across hierarchy levels. **Solution**: FoodCHA employs a progressive anchoring prediction strategy, where high-level categories guide subcategory identification, and subcategories guide cooking style recognition, thereby improving semantic consistency and attribute-level discrimination.
- **Challenge 2: Non-canonical outputs from VLMs hinder deployment**. Open-ended generation by VLMs can produce labels not aligned with standard taxonomies, complicating subsequent nutritional analysis and data standardization. **Solution**: FoodCHA restricts the recognition process to a fixed label space, uses the dataset hierarchy to constrain candidates at each stage, and normalizes free-form outputs to canonical labels, ensuring structured and ontology-compliant predictions.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation & Deployment**: FoodCHA is built on the lightweight Moondream-2B vision-language model, designed to reduce computational and memory overhead while maintaining strong reasoning capabilities, making it suitable for deployment on actual devices.
- **Evaluation Results**: Experiments were conducted on the FoodNExTDB dataset, which defines 10 high-level categories, 62 subcategories, and 9 cooking styles.
- **Comparison with Related Work**: Compared to Food-Llama-3.2-11B, FoodCHA improved category recognition precision by 13.8%, subcategory recognition precision by 38.2%, and achieved a striking 153.2% improvement in cooking style classification precision. This demonstrates that a well-designed agentic framework can outperform larger specialized models in fine-grained food analysis tasks, even with a significantly smaller parameter count.

## 4. Conclusion
FoodCHA effectively addresses the challenges of fine-grained attribute extraction and non-canonical labeling in food recognition by introducing a hierarchical agentic mechanism. It not only achieves significantly higher accuracy than existing large vision-language models but also enhances practical deployability through the use of a lightweight base model, offering a new technical pathway for real-time, accurate personal nutrition monitoring.

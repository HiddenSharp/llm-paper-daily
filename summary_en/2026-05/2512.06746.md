## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Vision Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly adopted for AI-generated image (AIGI) detection. However, converting VLMs into reliable detectors is resource-intensive, and the resulting models often suffer from hallucinations and poor generalization.
- **Why Existing Work Fails**: Current methods often force a single model to handle both semantic and pixel-level features, ignoring the inherent strengths of different architectures. Empirical analysis shows that fine-tuning VLMs with pixel-artifact supervision leads to weak generalization because VLMs lack inductive bias for low-level artifacts. Conversely, conventional vision models capture pixel artifacts well but are insensitive to semantic inconsistencies. This "task-model misalignment" causes systematic detection failures.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed**: The "Task-Model Alignment" principle and its instantiation, AlignGemini.
- **Challenge 1: Lack of inductive bias in VLMs for low-level pixel artifacts.** VLMs are optimized for high-level semantic reasoning, making them inefficient and poorly generalizing when forced to learn pixel-level artifacts. The paper addresses this by decomposing AIGI detection into two orthogonal subtasks and training the VLM branch with pure semantic supervision, allowing it to focus on its strength: semantic consistency checking.
- **Challenge 2: Generalization bottlenecks caused by single models failing to balance semantic and pixel cues.** Neglecting either subtask leads to blind spots. The paper introduces a specialized conventional vision model branch trained with pure pixel-artifact supervision to complement the VLM. By enforcing specialization, AlignGemini maximizes complementary discrimination across both dimensions, achieving strong generalization.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation**: AlignGemini is a two-branch detector. One branch is a VLM trained with pure semantic supervision, and the other is a vision model expert trained with pure pixel-artifact supervision. The method avoids complex annotations, reinforcement learning, or carefully designed Chain-of-Thought (CoT) supervision, significantly simplifying training data construction.
- **Evaluation Results**: On in-the-wild benchmarks, AlignGemini improved average accuracy by 9.5%. It achieved robust performance even with simplified training data.
- **Comparison with Related Work**: AlignGemini consistently outperforms existing detectors across five datasets. Furthermore, it achieves superior generalization while substantially reducing training data complexity, demonstrating that generalization in AIGI detection stems from aligning supervision with model inductive biases rather than merely scaling data volume or diversity.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper reveals the fundamental "task-model misalignment" in AIGI detection, where VLMs excel at semantics but struggle with pixels, while traditional vision models show the opposite trend. By proposing the "Task-Model Alignment" principle, the authors constructed AlignGemini, which decouples detection into semantic consistency checking and pixel-artifact detection, handled by the most suitable model branches respectively. This approach significantly improves detection accuracy and generalization with simplified training data, providing a new principled framework for designing efficient and generalizable AIGI detectors.

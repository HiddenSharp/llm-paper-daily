## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Classical learning theory focuses on correctness and generalization for predictors. However, for executable procedures like algorithm solvers, correctness is insufficient; two solvers may both be correct on a deployment distribution but differ substantially in runtime.
- **Why Existing Work Falls Short**: Current approaches often treat LLMs as black-box code generators or focus solely on solution quality, ignoring the generalization of computational efficiency. They fail to leverage latent structures within task distributions to optimize runtime, resulting in code that lacks distribution-specific computational optimizations.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed**: A distribution-aware algorithm design framework centered on the abstraction of a "Solver Hint"—reusable structure inferred from samples and compiled into specialized solver code.
- **Challenge 1: How to ensure the generated solver is both correct and efficient on unknown distributions?**
  - **Difficulty**: Average-case complexity analysis typically requires closed-form descriptions of distributions, which are often unknown and complex in practical deployments.
  - **Solution**: The paper proves that the empirically fastest sample-consistent solver from a fixed library generalizes in both correctness and runtime, and that statistically identifiable hints can be recovered and compiled from polynomially many samples.
- **Challenge 2: How to enable LLM Agents to effectively discover and utilize these distribution-specific structures?**
  - **Difficulty**: LLMs generating complete solvers in one shot tend to produce generic, inefficient code and struggle to spontaneously find computational shortcuts for specific data distributions.
  - **Solution**: The framework redefines the LLM Agent's role to proposing structural hypotheses and estimating reusable summaries. By identifying "solver hints" (e.g., backdoor sets in SAT, latent decompositions in graphs), the agent compiles these into specialized code, replacing ambient exponential search with distribution-specific computation.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation**: The framework was instantiated with LLM code agents across 21 structured combinatorial optimization target distributions spanning seven problem classes.
- **Evaluation Results**:
  - **Quality**: Synthesized solvers achieved a mean normalized quality of 0.971, improving by +0.224 over the average heuristic pool and +0.098 over the highest-quality heuristic.
  - **Speed**: The solvers were 336.9× faster than the quality-best heuristic, 342.8× faster than Gurobi, and 16.1× faster than the selected time-limited exact backend.
  - **PACE 2025 Performance**: On released PACE 2025 Dominating Set private instances, the synthesized solver was valid on all 100 graphs and ran approximately two orders of magnitude faster than top competition solvers, with a moderate quality gap.
- **Comparison**: Compared to traditional general-purpose optimizers (like Gurobi) and single high-quality heuristics, this method achieves superior speed and quality by changing the computational scale—replacing general-purpose optimization with compiled, distribution-specific computation.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper introduces a novel LLM Agent-based approach for distribution-aware algorithm design. By introducing "Solver Hints," it shifts the learning focus from mere correctness to the generalization of computational efficiency. Theoretical proofs establish recoverability and generalization bounds with polynomial samples, while experiments demonstrate significant outperformance over existing heuristics and commercial solvers, particularly achieving order-of-magnitude speedups by leveraging distribution-specific structures.

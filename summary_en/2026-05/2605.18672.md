## 1. What the Paper Does
- **Background**: The deployment of LLM agents in safety-critical settings poses a qualitatively new challenge for runtime assurance. Unlike conventional software, LLM agents combine non-deterministic reasoning with closed-loop execution, where generated plans influence the physical world or system states, amplifying the consequences of errors.
- **Limitations of Existing Work**: Current single-layer safety enforcement mechanisms (such as prompt engineering, fine-tuning, or single-layer monitoring) are structurally insufficient. This is because the three dimensions of safe operation—semantic intent/policy compliance, environmental validity, and dynamical feasibility—depend on distinct information sets available at different execution stages. Collapsing these into a single abstraction layer inevitably leaves at least one dimension uncertified, a structural flaw inherent to agent execution rather than a contingent limitation of current systems.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed a Three-Layer Probabilistic Assume-Guarantee Architecture**: This architecture uses contract-based design to assign safety responsibilities to three independently certified layers. Each layer's probabilistic guarantee satisfies the assumptions of the next, allowing the derivation of compositional system-level safety bounds via the chain rule of probability.
- **Challenge 1: Heterogeneity of Safety Dimensions and Temporal Information Dependency**. The difficulty lies in verifying semantic compliance, environmental validity, and dynamical feasibility at different stages (instruction parsing, pre-interaction, and action execution), as all information is not available simultaneously. The paper addresses this by stratifying the architecture: the User Layer handles semantic intent, the Operational Layer validates environmental context, and the Functional Layer ensures dynamical feasibility,串联 them sequentially.
- **Challenge 2: Quantifying System-Level Safety under Non-Determinism**. The challenge is that LLMs' probabilistic outputs make traditional formal verification difficult, and errors may accumulate across layers. The paper solves this using probabilistic assume-guarantee contracts: defining probability bounds for each layer and calculating the composite safety upper bound for the entire execution chain using the probability chain rule.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation & Evaluation**: As a position paper, the focus is on theoretical framework construction rather than specific engineering implementation or large-scale benchmarking. The paper sketches the architectural blueprint and derives the compositional system-level safety bounds it admits.
- **Comparison with Related Work**: Unlike existing works focusing solely on content filtering or single-layer monitoring, this paper argues that empirical evaluation alone cannot establish the systematic assurance required for safety-critical deployment. It highlights gaps in current benchmarks regarding the open-ended failure space of LLM agents. The proposed method is theoretically superior to single-layer protections as it structurally addresses the time-varying availability of information.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper argues that single-layer safety enforcement is structurally inadequate for deployed LLM agents. The authors advocate for a contract-based three-layer architecture that independently certifies safety across semantic, environmental, and dynamic dimensions. While the framework provides theoretical compositional safety bounds, three open problems remain before it can become a deployable standard: estimating bounds from non-i.i.d. traces, ensuring graceful degradation of contracts under deployment drift, and extending the approach to multi-agent settings.

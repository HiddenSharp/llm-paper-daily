## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) is the dominant standard for healthcare data exchange, where electronic health records (EHR) form a directed graph of resources. Answering clinically meaningful questions requires agents to perform multi-step reasoning, filtering, and aggregation across multiple resource types.
- **Why Prior Work Falls Short**: Previous studies show that even tool-augmented LLM agents (using retrieval, code execution, or multi-turn planning) often select incorrect resources or violate traversal constraints, failing to handle complex structured clinical graphs effectively.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed**: A reinforcement learning (RL) post-training pipeline for optimizing tool-calling agents (CodeAct agent) performing multi-turn reasoning on FHIR structured graphs.
- **Challenge 1: Complex Multi-hop Graph Navigation and Resource Selection Errors**. The difficulty lies in clinical information being distributed across linked nodes, causing agents to get lost or choose wrong paths. The paper addresses this by framing FHIR reasoning as a sequential decision-making problem over a queryable structured graph and using RL post-training to optimize the agent's multi-step decision strategy.
- **Challenge 2: Data Integrity Constraints and Sparse Execution-Grounded Rewards**. The challenge is ensuring agent operations comply with medical data logic while obtaining effective feedback. The paper introduces an LLM Judge to provide execution-based reward signals and enforces data integrity constraints during RL training, ensuring queries are both correct and compliant.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation**: The authors built an end-to-end post-training pipeline including environment building, harness construction, model training, and custom evaluation. They implemented a multi-turn CodeAct agent trained with RL using a custom harness and tools.
- **Evaluation Results**: On the FHIR-AgentBench benchmark, the approach improved answer correctness from 50% (o4-mini baseline) to 77%.
- **Comparison**: Compared to prompt-based closed-model baselines, the RL post-trained smaller and cheaper Qwen3-8B model achieved superior performance while better adhering to data integrity constraints.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper addresses the complex reasoning challenges in FHIR medical data graphs by proposing a solution combining reinforcement learning with tool calling. By modeling clinical QA as a sequential decision problem and leveraging execution-based rewards for post-training, the method significantly enhances the multi-step reasoning capability and accuracy of small open-source models on real-world hospital data, providing an efficient and reliable agent framework for healthcare interoperability applications.

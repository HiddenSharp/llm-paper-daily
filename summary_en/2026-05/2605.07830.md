## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents in offensive cybersecurity tasks such as penetration testing and red-teaming. Current evaluations primarily focus on outcome-centric metrics, i.e., whether an agent successfully completes an attack.
- **Why Existing Work Falls Short**: Existing benchmarks (e.g., CTF-based or CVE exploitation) ignore the behavioral patterns of agents during attack execution. They fail to reveal systematic biases in how agents select attack strategies or quantify the impact of these biases on efficacy, leaving a gap in understanding agent characteristics and associated risks.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed CyBiasBench**: A comprehensive benchmark comprising 630 sessions to evaluate 5 agents across 3 targets, 4 prompt conditions, and 10 attack families, designed to systematically quantify attack-selection bias in LLM agents.
- **Challenge 1: How to accurately quantify agent preferences for specific attack types?**
  - **Difficulty**: Agent behavior is diverse and influenced by prompts, making it hard to distinguish random fluctuations from inherent biases.
  - **Solution**: The paper logs raw HTTP traffic and classifies requests using a deterministic classifier based on the OWASP Core Rule Set (CRS). By analyzing selection rate distributions and entropy, it precisely identifies that agents disproportionately concentrate efforts on a narrow subset of attack families.
- **Challenge 2: Can this bias be easily corrected via prompting, and does it affect success rates?**
  - **Difficulty**: Determining whether the bias is superficial or a deep-seated trait, and whether forcing behavioral change yields benefits.
  - **Solution**: Experiments reveal a "bias momentum effect," where agents resist explicit steering toward attack families conflicting with their bias. The study finds that forced distribution shifts do not yield measurable improvements in attack performance, proving that bias is an inherent agent trait rather than a primary driver of success.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Implementation & Deployment**: The authors released an interactive result dashboard and reproducibility artifacts, including aggregated session-level statistics and full evaluation scripts, to ensure reproducibility.
- **Evaluation Results**:
  - Tested on 5 mainstream LLM agents (Claude, Codex, Gemini, GLM, Kimi).
  - Results show distinct dominant attack families and varying entropy levels for each agent, confirming explicit attack-selection bias.
  - The bias persists across different prompt conditions, indicating it is a stable trait.
  - Attempts to force agents to change their attack distribution via prompts encountered strong resistance (bias momentum), with no significant improvement in attack success rates.
- **Comparison**: Unlike previous outcome-oriented benchmarks that only check if a target is breached, CyBiasBench analyzes the "how" of attacks, exposing issues of strategic singularity and behavioral inertia overlooked by prior work.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper systematically reveals and quantifies "attack-selection bias" in LLM cybersecurity agents. By introducing CyBiasBench, the authors demonstrate that different agents possess unique, stable attack preferences that exhibit inertia and are resistant to simple prompt engineering corrections. Furthermore, this bias does not directly determine attack success. These findings are crucial for understanding the behavioral risks of LLM agents, optimizing automated red-teaming strategies, and establishing more comprehensive AI security governance standards.

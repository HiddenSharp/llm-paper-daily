## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Context window expansion in Large Language Models (LLMs) is often viewed as a straightforward capability upgrade. Classical game theory suggests that longer interaction histories should facilitate sustained cooperation in multi-agent social dilemmas (e.g., Prisoner's Dilemma).
- **Why Existing Work Falls Short**: Prior approaches overlook the structural difference between human cognition (which uses forgetting as a mechanism for forgiveness) and LLMs (which process dense, verbatim histories). Existing methods fail to explain why perfect historical ledgers might doom systems to unforgiving retaliation and do not address how expanded memory specifically erodes cooperative intent.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed the "Memory Curse" phenomenon and its mechanistic analysis**: The paper identifies that expanding accessible history systematically degrades cooperation in multi-agent social dilemmas across various LLMs and games.
- **Challenge 1: The root cause of cooperation collapse is the erosion of forward-looking intent, not just paranoia or memory length.**
  - **Difficulty**: It is challenging to distinguish whether the computational burden of length or the psychological impact of content causes the failure.
  - **Solution**: Lexical analysis of 378,000 reasoning traces links the breakdown to reduced forward-looking intent. This is validated by a cognitive probe: a LoRA adapter fine-tuned exclusively on forward-looking traces mitigates the decay and transfers zero-shot to distinct games.
- **Challenge 2: Explicit Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning paradoxically amplifies the memory curse.**
  - **Difficulty**: CoT is generally expected to improve reasoning, but in long-memory contexts, detailed deliberation exacerbates negative interpretations of past betrayals.
  - **Solution**: Ablation studies show that removing explicit CoT often reduces the collapse. Furthermore, "memory sanitization" experiments (replacing visible history with synthetic cooperative records while keeping prompt length fixed) restore cooperation, proving that the trigger is the specific content of memory interacting with reasoning patterns, not length alone.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Experimental Setup**: Evaluated across 7 LLMs and 4 games (Prisoner's Dilemma, Traveler's Dilemma, Public Goods, Trust Game) over 500 rounds.
- **Key Results**: In 18 out of 28 model-game settings, expanding accessible history degraded cooperation, confirming the prevalence of the "Memory Curse."
- **Comparisons and Analysis**:
  - **Short vs. Long Memory**: Agents with short memory (e.g., History Length=2) reciprocated recent cooperation and sustained trust. Agents with long memory (e.g., History Length=80) dug up distant defections, adopting grudge-holding strategies that led to cooperation collapse.
  - **Intervention Effects**:
    1. **Targeted Fine-tuning**: LoRA adapters trained on forward-looking traces significantly restored cooperation.
    2. **Memory Sanitization**: Replacing negative historical content with synthetic cooperative records substantially restored cooperation, isolating content as the key driver.
    3. **CoT Ablation**: Removing explicit CoT reasoning alleviated the memory curse in many cases, indicating that excessive deliberation is harmful in long-memory contexts.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper reveals a counter-intuitive phenomenon in LLM multi-agent systems: the "Memory Curse." It demonstrates that simply expanding context windows does not guarantee better social coordination; instead, it can undermine cooperation by causing agents to over-focus on historical negatives and amplify them through detailed reasoning. Through lexical analysis, targeted fine-tuning, and memory sanitization, the study shows that cooperation collapse stems from eroded forward-looking intent and the amplification of negative memories by explicit reasoning. These findings recast memory as an active determinant of behavior, suggesting that long recall can either destabilize or support cooperation depending on the elicited reasoning patterns. This has significant implications for designing autonomous systems, hinting at the need for human-like "forgetting" or abstraction mechanisms to promote social welfare.

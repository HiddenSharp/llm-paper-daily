## 1. What the Paper Did
- **Background**: Tool calling is a cornerstone of modern Agentic AI systems. However, prominent benchmarks (e.g., Confetti, When2Call) are text-based, while real-world applications like customer support increasingly rely on voice interactions.
- **Why Existing Work Falls Short**: Existing audio function-calling benchmarks require building new audio corpora, defining new tools, and annotating new labels from scratch. This is costly and domain-specific, making it difficult for organizations with validated text benchmarks to evaluate how their specific tasks perform in voice deployments or to quantify the performance degradation from text to speech.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed a dataset-agnostic framework**: A system that systematically converts verified text-based tool-calling benchmarks into speech benchmarks without re-annotating tool schemas or gold labels, enabling reproducible and verifiable paired text-audio evaluation.
- **Challenge 1: How to generate high-quality audio evaluation data while preserving original annotations?**
  - **Difficulty**: Direct recording or synthesis often requires significant manual intervention to align with original intents and control acoustic variables.
  - **Solution**: The framework uses Text-to-Speech (TTS), speaker variation, and environmental noise simulation to create paired text-audio instances, strictly preserving the original dataset's annotations and tool schemas.
- **Challenge 2: How to accurately quantify and diagnose performance degradation in multimodal models for voice tool calling?**
  - **Difficulty**: The black-box nature of end-to-end multimodal models makes it hard to distinguish between ASR errors and tool-calling logic errors, with varying sensitivity to acoustic features across models.
  - **Solution**: Extensive evaluation of 7 omni-modal models on audio-converted Confetti and When2Call datasets reveals model- and task-dependent performance. The "text-to-voice" gap is quantified, and failure analysis shows degradations mostly stem from misunderstandings of argument values in speech.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Experimental Setup**: Evaluated 7 omni-modal models on audio versions of Confetti and When2Call. Included text-only baselines, ambiguity-based reformulation stress tests, and a reference-free LLM-as-judge protocol validated against human preferences.
- **Key Results**:
  - Performance is highly model- and task-dependent: Gemini-3.1-Flash-Live scored highest on Confetti (70.4), while GPT-Realtime-1.5 performed best on When2Call (71.9).
  - Text-to-voice gap: Ranged from 1.8 points for Qwen3-Omni to 4.8 points for GPT-Realtime-1.5 on Confetti.
  - Failure Analysis: Degradations primarily reflect misunderstandings of argument values in speech.
- **LLM-as-Judge Validity**: Open-source Qwen3 judges with ≥8B parameters showed >80% agreement with proprietary judges, supporting privacy-preserving evaluation.
- **Comparison Advantage**: Unlike traditional audio benchmarks requiring new corpus construction, this framework offers a verifiable, low-cost diagnostic tool that leverages existing high-quality text benchmarks, enhancing reproducibility and accessibility.

## 4. Conclusion
This paper introduces a general framework for converting text tool-calling benchmarks into audio benchmarks, addressing the lack of generality and reusability in existing audio benchmarks. Empirical studies reveal performance bottlenecks and failure modes of omni-modal models in voice tool calling and validate the reliability of open-source LLMs as evaluators. The framework provides a cost-effective, reproducible diagnostic method for voice agent development, complementing specialized audio corpora.

## 1. What the Paper Does
- **Background**: LLM-based multi-agent simulation is a powerful testbed for studying social opinion dynamics. However, current approaches typically fall into two contrasting categories: relying on fixed update rules with limited cognitive grounding (traditional ABMs) or delegating belief change largely to unconstrained LLM interactions.
- **Why Existing Work Fails**: Traditional ABMs are too simplified to capture nuanced human reasoning. Existing LLM-based simulations (e.g., Generative Agents) treat belief updating as an emergent outcome of open-ended interaction, lacking structured representations of belief influence and cognitively grounded mechanisms for resistance to change. This leads to "over-smoothing," where agents converge too quickly to consensus, failing to preserve the persistent disagreement and bounded polarization observed in real human debates.

## Core Contributions
- **Proposed ScioMind**: A cognitively grounded simulation framework that bridges structured opinion dynamics with LLM-based agent reasoning.
- **Challenge 1: How to simulate cognitively realistic belief resistance and updating?**
  - **Difficulty**: LLMs are prone to arbitrarily changing stances based on recent interactions, lacking the psychological stability of human "anchoring," leading to unstable belief trajectories.
  - **Solution**: Introduced a **Memory-Anchored Belief Update Rule** that modulates susceptibility to influence via personality-conditioned anchoring strength, ensuring belief updates are both interactive and cognitively consistent.
- **Challenge 2: How to achieve long-term agent heterogeneity and behavioral realism?**
  - **Difficulty**: Existing works often use static profiles, failing to reflect the evolution of internal states through experience, resulting in insufficient opinion diversity and unstable oscillations.
  - **Solution**: Designed a **Hierarchical Memory Architecture** for persistent, experience-driven belief formation, combined with **Dynamic Agent Profiles** derived from a corpus-grounded retrieval pipeline. This enables heterogeneous personalities, rationales, and evolving internal states, enhancing behavioral realism and opinion diversity.

## Implementation and Evaluation
- **Evaluation Scenario**: Multiple case studies in a real-world policy debate scenario.
- **Metrics**: Polarization, diversity, extremization, and trajectory stability.
- **Results vs. Baselines**:
  - ScioMind's components consistently improved behavioral realism compared to baseline methods.
  - **Dynamic Profiles** significantly increased opinion diversity.
  - **Memory and Reflection** mechanisms effectively reduced unstable oscillations.
  - **Anchoring** induced more persistent belief trajectories, aligning better with patterns reported in political psychology and avoiding the rapid convergence and over-smoothing common in traditional LLM simulations.

## 4. Conclusion
ScioMind successfully addresses the lack of structured constraints and cognitive foundations in existing LLM social simulations by incorporating the anchoring effect from cognitive psychology, hierarchical memory, and dynamic profiles. The framework enhances both simulation stability and behavioral realism, offering a novel solution for studying belief formation, persistence, and evolution in computational social science.
